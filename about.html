About Hangjie Lin

Coastal researcher exploring marsh resilience and sediment dynamics

I am currently a Ph.D. student in the Department of Earth & Environment at Boston University. Under the mentorship of coastal scientists, I investigate how geomorphic and hydrodynamic processes shape tidal wetlands and intertidal flats. My research combines field observations, remote sensing and numerical modelling to understand how salt marshes and sedimentary environments respond to sea‑level rise, storms and human activities.

Before joining BU, I earned an M.S. in Marine Geoscience (2020–2023) and a B.S. in Oceanographic Science (2016–2020) from Nanjing University. My master’s work focused on the geomorphology and sediment dynamics of the Liyashan oyster reefs and on monitoring coastal turbidity variations using satellite imagery. These experiences fostered my interest in the interactions between physical processes and ecosystem health.

My current projects examine salt marsh resilience to sea‑level rise, forced evolution of tidal flats and multi‑scale trends in suspended‑sediment concentration. I investigate sampling bias in optical remote sensing of sediment and chlorophyll by designing controlled experiments and training spatiotemporal neural networks to reconstruct bias‑reduced fields. I also compile four‑decade SSC records along the U.S. East Coast to untangle long‑term trends and their drivers. Along the way I collect in situ data, develop remote‑sensing products and implement hydrodynamic models. I enjoy mentoring students and serving as a teaching fellow for courses on coastal processes and remote sensing.

Outside of research, I am an hiker and fitness enthusiasts and enjoy playing soccer with friends. I have been honoured with the 2025 Warren McLeod Fellowship and a FINESST fellowship in 2025, recognitions that support my efforts to advance coastal science.

Research Highlights

Salt Marsh Resilience

Salt marshes provide essential ecosystem services and act as natural buffers. I fuse in‑situ measurements, satellite imagery and meteorological data to quantify net sediment fluxes and evaluate pathways of marsh resilience and vulnerability under climate change.

  • Fusing USGS, Sentinel‑2/Landsat and wind/wave data to estimate net sediment flux
  • Compiling global marsh change and hydrodynamic drivers datasets
  • Developing multi‑indicator resilience/vulnerability frameworks

SSC & Chlorophyll‑a Bias

Satellite observations suffer from sampling gaps and survivorship bias. I design controlled experiments and spatiotemporal machine‑learning models to quantify and correct biases in suspended‑sediment and chlorophyll retrievals.

  • Quantifying survivorship bias using full vs satellite‑masked datasets
  • Training ConvLSTM/3D U‑Net models to reconstruct bias‑reduced fields
  • Mapping uncertainty as a function of sampling gaps and seasonality

SSC Trends in U.S. Coast

The suspended‑sediment climate of U.S. East Coast lagoons and bays is changing. I build a harmonised multi‑decadal record of SSC proxies to reveal trends, separate inner‑bay and shelf signals and identify the drivers behind observed changes.

  • Harmonising 1984–2024 SSC proxy records across barrier‑island systems
  • Separating inner‑bay versus shelf contributions
  • Attributing trends to wave climate, inlet morphology, dredging, sea‑level rise and watershed loads

Tidal Flats Evolution

Coastal engineering and changing boundary conditions modify wave and sediment dynamics. Through numerical experiments I investigate how seawalls and reclamation projects force the evolution of tidal flats and alter their equilibrium profiles.

  • Simulating hydrodynamic and sediment responses to seawalls
  • Characterising equilibrium profile adjustments under altered forcing
  • Exploring interactions between tidal flats and engineered structures
